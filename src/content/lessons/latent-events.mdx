    ```lua
RegisterNetEvent('qu_latent_events:upload', function(payload)
    local src = source
    local size = #payload
    print('[qu_latent_events] server received ' .. size .. ' bytes from player ' .. src)

    -- Acknowledge back down to the client, also as a latent stream.
    TriggerLatentClientEvent('qu_latent_events:ack', src, 25000, size)
end)
    ```

    Open `client.lua` and paste this:

    ```lua
RegisterCommand('latenttest', function()

end, false)

    print('[qu_latent_events] client got ack: server saw ' .. size .. ' bytes')
end)
    ```

## How it works

The proof worked, but the point of this lesson is one number: `25000`. Everything else is plumbing you have seen before. So we take it apart in the order the data actually travels: out of the client, across the wire, into the server, and back.

### Why a regular event can time a player out

When you call `TriggerServerEvent('x', bigTable)`, FiveM serializes the whole payload and pushes it onto the player's reliable network channel as one unit. The channel will not move on to anything else until that whole unit is delivered. For a 200-byte table you never notice. For a multi-megabyte blob, that one event hogs the channel, every other packet for that player queues up behind it, and on a slow or lossy connection the client can stall past its timeout window and get dropped. The player did nothing wrong. Your event ate their connection.

Latent events fix this by chunking. Instead of one big push, FiveM breaks the payload into pieces and feeds them onto the channel at a rate you choose, leaving gaps for everything else. The payload still arrives whole, in order, and triggers the same handler. It just arrives as a polite trickle instead of a flood.

### The bps argument, the one number that matters

Compare the two calls. A regular event takes the name and the data:

```lua
TriggerServerEvent('qu_latent_events:upload', payload)
```

The latent version inserts one extra argument between them:

```lua
TriggerLatentServerEvent('qu_latent_events:upload', 25000, payload)
```

That `25000` is the **bytes-per-second stream rate**. It is not a size, not a timeout, not a chunk count. It is a speed limit. It tells FiveM: never push this payload onto the channel faster than 25,000 bytes every second. The data is metered out at that ceiling until it is all delivered.

Every latent native takes the rate in the same slot, right after the target arguments:

- `TriggerLatentServerEvent(eventName, bps, ...)` — client to server.
- `TriggerLatentClientEvent(eventName, target, bps, ...)` — server to client, where `target` is the player server id.

In our code the client streams up at `25000` and the server streams the ack back down at `25000`. Same number, same meaning, opposite directions.

### How to size bps for a payload

Once you know the rate is a speed limit, sizing it is just division. Transfer time is payload size divided by rate:

```text
transfer time (seconds) = payload size (bytes) / bps
```

Our 20,000-byte payload at 25,000 bps takes 20000 / 25000 = 0.8 seconds. That is why the server line lands about a second after the client line, not instantly.

Now you can reason about real numbers. A 5 MB character export (roughly 5,000,000 bytes) at 25,000 bps would take 200 seconds. That is almost certainly too slow, so you raise the rate. At 500,000 bps the same export finishes in 10 seconds. The trade is simple: a higher rate finishes faster but uses more of the channel, which is the exact congestion you were trying to avoid. A lower rate is gentler but slower. Pick the largest rate that still leaves headroom for normal gameplay traffic, and bias lower when the payload is huge or the players are far away. There is no single correct value. The number depends on your payload size and how much delay your feature can tolerate.

<Checkpoint question="A teammate sets bps to 20000 and expects a 20,000-byte payload to arrive instantly. Why does it take a full second, and what single change makes it arrive in a tenth of a second?">
  The bps argument is a speed limit, not a size, so a 20,000-byte payload at 20,000 bps is metered out over 20000 / 20000 = 1 second by design. The data is correct, it is just deliberately slow. To finish in roughly a tenth of a second, raise the rate to 200000 bps (20000 / 200000 = 0.1 seconds). Nothing else changes. The payload is identical and the handler is identical. The only knob is the rate, and a higher rate trades channel headroom for speed.
</Checkpoint>

### The broadcast trap

This is the gotcha that bites people in production. With a regular client event you broadcast to everyone by passing `-1` as the target:

```lua
TriggerClientEvent('announcement', -1, 'Server restarting')
```

`TriggerLatentClientEvent` accepts `-1` too, and it looks harmless:

```lua
-- Dangerous on a full server with a big payload
TriggerLatentClientEvent('qu_latent_events:ack', -1, 25000, hugePayload)
```

But latent broadcasting does not share one 25,000 bps stream across the server. It opens a separate metered stream to every connected player, each at the full rate you named. On a 64-slot server that is 64 simultaneous streams at 25,000 bps, which is 1.6 MB per second leaving your server's uplink the moment you fire it. Send a large payload to `-1` and you can saturate the very channel latent events exist to protect, hitting every player at once.

The rule: latent broadcasts are for one target, or a small known list of targets. If you genuinely must push a big payload to everyone, stream it to one player at a time in a loop with a delay between sends, or rethink whether every player actually needs the data. Most of the time they do not. That is why our ack streams back to a single `src`, never to `-1`.

### When you reach for this pattern

Reach for a latent event the moment a payload is big enough that a single burst could stall a connection. Three honest cases:

- **Character or inventory exports.** A full character blob with appearance, inventory, and metadata can be hundreds of kilobytes. Stream it instead of bursting it.
- **Image or buffer uploads from NUI.** Screenshots, signatures, or generated images are large binary strings. Latent keeps the upload from freezing the player mid-send.
- **Server-to-client data sync on join.** Pushing a large config or world-state table to a freshly connected player is exactly when their connection is most fragile. Latent gives it time to land.

For everything small, a chat line, a notification, a single number, stay with the regular `TriggerServerEvent` and `TriggerClientEvent`. The rule of thumb holds: small is regular, big is latent.

## Try it yourself

<Exercise>
  Make the bps cost visible. Right now you cannot feel the difference between a fast rate and a slow one because the payload is tiny. Time a real transfer.

  Capture the start time on the client, then have the server stamp arrival and the ack carry the duration back:

  ```lua
  -- client.lua
  local uploadStartedAt = 0

  RegisterCommand('latenttest', function()
      local payload = string.rep('A', 500000) -- 500 KB now, not 20 KB
      uploadStartedAt = GetGameTimer()
      TriggerLatentServerEvent('qu_latent_events:upload', 25000, payload)
      print('[qu_latent_events] upload started (' .. #payload .. ' bytes at 25000 bps)')
  end, false)

  RegisterNetEvent('qu_latent_events:ack', function(size)
      local elapsed = GetGameTimer() - uploadStartedAt
      print('[qu_latent_events] round trip for ' .. size .. ' bytes took ' .. elapsed .. ' ms')
  end)
  ```

  Run `/latenttest` and read the elapsed time. A 500 KB upload at 25,000 bps should take roughly 20 seconds up, plus the ack stream back down. Now change only the bps number on the `TriggerLatentServerEvent` line to `250000` and run it again. The same payload should land in roughly a tenth of the time. You just measured the one parameter this lesson is about, instead of trusting the math on paper. Then ask yourself: at which rate did other gameplay traffic start to feel choppy? That is your real ceiling.
</Exercise>
