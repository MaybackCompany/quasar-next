MLO and map optimization for server performance

By the end of this lesson you will analyze an MLO interior for common performance problems — texture budget, draw calls, collision cost, LOD issues — and apply three fixes that measurably improve client FPS.

You'll ship

Time

You'll learn

Prereqs
You have a FiveM server with at least one custom MLO installed. Basic knowledge of 3D model concepts (vertices, textures, draw calls) helps.
In 3 bullets

You will optimize one MLO by doing this:

  • Open the MLO in CodeWalker or a 3D tool and count its draw calls, texture memory, and polygon count.
  • Identify the three biggest performance offenders: oversized textures (4K on a wall), high-poly collision meshes, and geometry the player never sees.
  • Apply fixes: resize textures to 1024x1024, simplify collision to a box mesh, and delete faces inside walls and ceilings.

Why it matters

Unoptimized MLOs are the number one cause of client FPS drops on FiveM servers. One bad interior near a spawn point can make every new player think your server runs at 25 FPS.

Vocabulary

Draw call
One command from the CPU to the GPU to render a mesh with a specific material. More draw calls = more CPU work.
Example: a MLO with 500 draw calls is heavy. Under 100 is good.
Texture budget
The total video memory used by all textures in the scene. GTA V's engine struggles above ~3GB of texture memory on a 4GB GPU.
Example: five 4K textures consume ~80MB each of VRAM.
Collision mesh
A simplified 3D shape used for physics and bullet impacts, separate from the visual mesh. High-poly collision is wasteful.
Example: a detailed chair's collision should be a box, not the chair itself.

Common failures

  • FPS does not improve after texture resize: the bottleneck may be draw calls, not texture memory. Check the draw call count in CodeWalker — if it is above 300, focus on merging objects first.
  • MLO looks blurry after texture resize: you resized too aggressively. 1024x1024 is safe for most surfaces. Only resize textures that are on walls or floors — keep small detail textures like labels at their original resolution.
  • Collision breaks after simplification: your new collision box is the wrong size or position. In CodeWalker, use the collision view to align the box primitive with the visual mesh before exporting.
  • Map fails to load after editing: you broke the .ytyp file's structure. Always work on a copy, and validate the YTYP file with CodeWalker's built-in validator before loading on the server.

Cheat sheet

MLO optimization targets

  • Draw calls. Under 150 for a small interior (1-2 rooms). Under 300 for a large interior (5+ rooms).
  • Texture resolution. 1024x1024 for walls and floors. 512x512 for small props. 128x128 for tiny details.
  • Collision polys. Less than 20% of visual poly count. Box and cylinder primitives wherever possible.
  • Unseen faces. Zero. Delete any face inside a wall, ceiling, or floor.

The big idea, in one minute

GTA V's engine has limits. When a player walks near your custom MLO, the game loads every texture, draws every face, and calculates collisions for every object — even the ones the player cannot see. Texture budget is the total video memory consumed. Draw calls are the CPU commands to render each material. Collision cost is the physics calculation for every triangle in the collision mesh. Reduce all three and FPS goes up.

Think of a warehouse full of boxes.

The warehouse (MLO) has 10,000 boxes (polygons). Every time someone walks in, the system counts all 10,000 boxes — even the ones stacked behind walls where nobody looks. Textures are like wrapping paper: 10 rolls of expensive gold foil (4K textures) cost more than 10 rolls of plain paper (1K textures). Collision is checking whether someone bumped into each box — it is faster to check 10 big boxes (simplified collision) than 10,000 small ones.

Your optimization is clearing the boxes nobody sees, using cheaper wrapping paper, and replacing 10,000 small boxes with 10 big ones.

Bad way and good way, side by side

Most MLO creators ship 4K textures and full-detail collision because it looks good in screenshots. Players pay the cost in FPS.

Don't do this

Shipping an MLO with 4K textures, full-detail collision, and hidden geometry.

Players near this interior lose 15-25 FPS. On a full server, 64 players near spawn all suffer. The server feels laggy even though server CPU is fine — it is every client's GPU struggling with the MLO.

Do this

Resize textures, merge materials, simplify collision, and delete unseen faces.

From normal gameplay distance (3-5 meters), the visual difference is invisible. But FPS near the interior jumps 15-20 frames. Players notice the smoothness, not the texture resolution on a wall they sprint past.

Step 1

Measure baseline FPS

You cannot improve what you do not measure.

  1. Join your server and enable FPS display: F8 console, type +showfps.
  2. Stand at least 50 meters from the MLO. Note the FPS number. This is your "ambient" baseline.
  3. Walk directly up to the MLO entrance. Stand still for 5 seconds. Note the FPS number.
  4. Enter the MLO. Stand still for 5 seconds. Note the FPS number.
  5. Calculate the FPS drop: ambient minus inside. If the drop is less than 5 FPS, your MLO is already well optimized. If 10+ FPS, proceed.
Step 2

Open the MLO in CodeWalker and audit draw calls

CodeWalker shows exactly what the game engine sees.

  1. Open CodeWalker and load your MLO's .ytyp file.
  2. Select the interior archetype and open the Info panel (right-click → Info).
  3. Note the total draw call count. Each unique material-texture combo is a draw call.
  4. If draw calls exceed 150 for a small interior, look for objects that share textures and can be merged.
  5. Export the MLO as a backup before making any changes.
Step 3

Audit textures and resize oversized ones

4K textures on walls are wasted. Players sprint past them.

  1. In CodeWalker, open the Texture Browser for the MLO. Sort by resolution, largest first.
  2. Flag every texture over 2048x2048. These are candidates for resize.
  3. For each flagged texture, ask: "Will a player ever stare at this from 10cm away?" If not, resize to 1024x1024.
  4. Use ImageMagick, Photoshop, or any image editor to resize. Keep the original files as backup.
  5. Re-import the resized textures into the .ytd file and replace the MLO's texture dictionary.
Step 4

Simplify collision meshes

Collision does not need detail — it needs speed.

  1. In CodeWalker, enable collision view (View → Show Collision).
  2. Compare the collision mesh to the visual mesh. If they look identical, the collision is too detailed.
  3. For walls and floors: replace detailed collision with a simple box primitive that matches the bounds.
  4. For furniture: replace chair/table collision meshes with bounding boxes or cylinders.
  5. Save the optimized collision as a new .ybn file and update the MLO's archetype to reference it.
Step 5

Delete unseen faces and re-measure FPS

Faces inside walls and ceilings are pure waste.

  1. In CodeWalker, orbit the camera inside walls and above ceilings. Look for polygons that face away from the playable area.
  2. Select and delete any face the player can never see — backs of wall panels, undersides of floors, tops of ceilings above the playable area.
  3. Save the optimized MLO files. Restart the resource on your server.
  4. Stand in the same spot as your baseline measurement. Check FPS.
  5. Calculate improvement: new inside FPS minus old inside FPS. Document both numbers.
Recap

Three tests, in order

Run each test and confirm the expected behavior. If something does not match, fix it before moving on.

  1. Test 1, texture memory reduction.
    • Action: after resizing textures, check the .ytd file size.
    • Expected: the .ytd file is at least 50% smaller than before.
    • How to verify: compare file sizes with ls -lh before and after.
  2. Test 2, collision still works.
    • Action: walk into every wall, jump on every piece of furniture, shoot the walls.
    • Expected: you cannot walk through walls. Bullets hit surfaces. Furniture blocks movement.
    • How to verify: if you fall through the floor, your collision box is too small or misaligned.
  3. Test 3, FPS improvement.
    • Action: stand in the exact same spot as your baseline measurement, inside the MLO.
    • Expected: FPS is higher than the baseline by at least 5 frames.
    • How to verify: if FPS did not improve, the bottleneck may be something else — check for script loops running inside the MLO, or excessive lighting/shadow casting.
What if FPS does not improve after all three fixes?

The bottleneck may not be the MLO itself. Check for: (1) script loops running every frame inside the MLO coordinates, (2) excessive dynamic lights or shadow-casting lights, (3) too many props spawned by scripts, (4) the MLO is loaded as multiple separate archetypes instead of one merged interior. Run resmon in the server console to see which resource is using the most CPU time near the MLO location.

The rules you just followed

  • Measure FPS before and after every change. Optimization without measurement is guessing.
  • Resize textures to 1024x1024 max for walls and floors. Only use higher resolutions for hero props players inspect closely.
  • Replace detailed collision meshes with box and cylinder primitives. Physics does not care about detail.
  • Delete every face the player cannot see — inside walls, under floors, above ceilings.
  • Always work on a copy of the MLO files. A broken .ytyp file can crash the entire map resource.
